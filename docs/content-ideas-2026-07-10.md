## Part 1 — Ten Concepts

### 1. The Drowned Archive
- **Id slug:** `the-drowned-archive`
- **Type:** case (3 acts)
- **Synopsis:** A lighter carrying an uncatalogued museum consignment — the estate of a dead East India Company hydrographer — founders in calm water off Wapping. The insurers hire the investigator when the salvage diver who recovered the crates refuses to go down again, then stops speaking altogether. Three crates reached the British Museum's receiving house; the manifest says there were four. Everyone who has handled the fourth crate's contents has begun keeping strange hours, and the receiving clerk has started writing his ledger entries right-to-left.
- **Central mystery question:** What was in the fourth crate, who took it, and why did a sound boat sink in still water?
- **Rational shadow:** Insurance fraud — the shipping agent scuttled the lighter to hide that the most valuable crate was sold off the books to a private collector; the diver's silence is a bought silence; the clerk is simply drinking.
- **Occult truth / Mythos connection:** The hydrographer's estate contained soundings and dredge-relics from a Thames-estuary survey that was suppressed in the 1850s — depth readings that describe a structure, not a riverbed. The relics *want* to return to the water. This is the strongest Mythos anchor among the main cases, but the case resolves fully as a smuggling/fraud plot; the estuary material is what the attentive player carries away.
- **Primary locations:** Wapping stairs and the salvage wharf; the British Museum receiving house; a Limehouse shipping agent's office; the diver's lodging; the Whispering Gallery (to trace the private sale).
- **Main factions:** Court of Smoke (fenced the crate), Rationalists Circle (a Fellow authenticated the estate and wants the survey suppressed *again*), Lamplighters (peripheral — the 1850s suppression was theirs).
- **Important NPCs:** Mr. Okonkwo-Hale, the salvage diver (mute by choice, not injury); Miss Adeline Ferrier, museum receiving clerk turned mirror-writer; Josiah Crumb, the shipping agent with two sets of books; Dr. Enfield Marsh, Rationalist hydrography Fellow, courteous and terrified; "The Ferryman", Court of Smoke fence who deals only afloat.
- **Clue ideas:** *(physical)* the lighter's dry rot is fresh-cut — bored from inside; *(physical)* the manifest page with a fourth entry scraped and rewritten; *(testimony)* a mudlark saw the crate rowed ashore before the sinking; *(testimony)* the diver's slate — he will write answers, never speak them; *(occult)* a dredge-relic: a basalt sounding-weight carved with an angular mark that is always cold; *(occult)* Ferrier's ledger — the mirrored entries are the 1850s soundings, which she has never seen; *(deduction-type clue)* the suppressed survey's file number in Lamplighter records; *(red herring)* Crumb's second ledger implicating a rival insurer — real fraud, wrong crime.
- **Key deductions:** (1) `da-scuttled-from-within` — dry rot + mudlark testimony ⇒ the sinking was staged after the crate was removed; (2) `da-survey-suppressed` — file number + Marsh's authentication ⇒ the estate was dangerous enough to bury twice; (3) `da-the-weight-returns` — sounding-weight + mirrored ledger ⇒ the relics are transcribing themselves through their handlers (gates the true ending).
- **Major branching choice:** The fourth crate is located intact on the Ferryman's barge. Sink it in the deep channel (what it wants — and the Lamplighters' preference), deliver it to the Rationalists for controlled destruction (Marsh's plea), or open it.
- **Faculty emphasis:** Perception and Reason lead; Vigor strong (diving, wharf work, a barge encounter); Lore for the relics; Influence for Crumb and the Ferryman; Nerve for the receiving-house night scenes.
- **Archetype opportunities:** Operator — an underwater recovery scene others cannot attempt; Occultist — Veil Sight on the sounding-weight reveals the mark is a *depth*, not a symbol; Deductionist — reconstructing the scraped manifest; Mesmerist — getting the diver to speak one sentence aloud (he says the case's most chilling line).
- **Flags / reputation / unlocks:** `da-crate-sunk` / `da-crate-opened` persist; Court rep swing ±2 via the Ferryman; sets `mythos-estuary-survey` if the player reads the soundings (feeds the Mythos thread and unlocks vignette #7).
- **Why it adds something new:** First river/maritime case, first museum-institutional setting, first case where the object of the mystery is *information about geography* rather than a person or artifact per se.

### 2. The Ravenscroft Remedy
- **Id slug:** `the-ravenscroft-remedy`
- **Type:** case (3 acts)
- **Synopsis:** Ravenscroft Asylum has a new superintendent and, suddenly, a miracle: chronic patients — the ones committed after "seeing too much" — are being pronounced cured and released. Then one of the cured walks into the Thames with his pockets full of stones, smiling. His sister hires the investigator. Inside Ravenscroft, the cured wards are unnervingly serene, the treatment registry is sealed, and the superintendent's "galvanic quietus" therapy has a waiting list of wealthy families eager to have inconvenient relatives made pleasant.
- **Central mystery question:** What does the quietus treatment actually remove from a patient — and where does it go?
- **Rational shadow:** Medical fraud and embezzlement — the "cure" is a heroic dose of a proprietary sedative regimen; releases free up beds sold to paying families; the drowned man was simply relapsing. Every serene ward can be explained by chemistry.
- **Occult truth / Mythos connection:** The treatment genuinely works: it excises the memory of what the patient saw beyond the Veil. But excised memory is not destroyed — it is *collected*, and the superintendent is only the instrument. Faint Mythos echo only: several excised memories, transcribed in the sealed registry, describe the same drowned architecture (motif shared with case #1) though no two patients ever met.
- **Primary locations:** Ravenscroft Asylum (wards, treatment theatre, records room, the water-gate onto the Thames); the drowned man's lodgings; a Harley Street consulting room; the sister's home.
- **Main factions:** Hermetic Order of the Grey Dawn (the superintendent's silent patron), Lamplighters (half their retired informants are patients here), Rationalists Circle (endorsed the therapy publicly and cannot afford a scandal).
- **Important NPCs:** Dr. Silas Vane, superintendent — genuinely believes he is merciful; Matron Odile Persk, who keeps unofficial notes and unofficial loyalties; Thomas Weeks, a "cured" patient whose serenity cracks at the sound of bells; Mrs. Harrow, the drowned man's sister, more formidable than she first appears; Brother Ashe, Grey Dawn liaison who visits monthly to take away "the residue".
- **Clue ideas:** *(physical)* the drowned man's pockets — the stones are all from the asylum's water-gate; *(physical)* treatment invoices billing a nonexistent supplier for "vitreous vessels"; *(testimony)* Matron Persk's account of what patients say *during* the quietus; *(testimony)* Weeks recalling, for exactly four seconds, what he no longer knows; *(occult)* a vitreous vessel — cold, faintly resonant, heavier full than it looks; *(occult)* the sealed registry pages where six unacquainted patients drew the same flooded street; *(red herring)* the previous superintendent's grievance letters alleging Vane bought his post — true, and irrelevant; *(physical)* Vane's own dosage log, showing he has begun treating himself.
- **Key deductions:** (1) `rr-cure-is-removal` — Persk testimony + Weeks's four seconds ⇒ the treatment removes rather than heals; (2) `rr-residue-carried` — invoices + vessel ⇒ the removed material is stored and collected by the Grey Dawn; (3) `rr-vane-a-patient` — dosage log + his serenity ⇒ Vane has excised his own knowledge of what he serves (gates the true ending).
- **Major branching choice:** The final treatment session is scheduled — the patient is a Lamplighter informant whose excised memory would name names the player needs. Halt the treatment and expose Ravenscroft (dooming the genuinely relieved patients back to their terrors), let it proceed and intercept the vessel, or submit to a partial quietus yourself to read what the collected residue holds (heavy Composure cost, unique clue).
- **Faculty emphasis:** Nerve and Influence lead; Reason for the paper trail; Perception on the wards; Lore for the vessels; Vigor kept relevant via an orderlies encounter and the water-gate.
- **Archetype opportunities:** Mesmerist — talk Weeks *around* his excision rather than through it; Occultist — Veil Sight shows the cured wards as the emptiest places in London; Deductionist — the invoice/bed-ledger reconciliation; Operator — the water-gate infiltration after hours.
- **Flags / reputation / unlocks:** `rr-ravenscroft-exposed` / `rr-vessel-taken` persist; Grey Dawn rep ±2; Rationalists −1 if exposed publicly. Sets `mythos-shared-drawing` if the registry pages are read. Unlocks vignette #9.
- **Why it adds something new:** Activates Ravenscroft from the design bible; first case whose moral dilemma is *whether the supernatural cure is worse than the mundane disease*; first Grey Dawn-fronted main case.

### 3. The Hollow Chime
- **Id slug:** `the-hollow-chime`
- **Type:** case (3 acts)
- **Synopsis:** Across three parishes, church bells have begun ringing the hour wrong — always the same wrong hour, always 3 a.m. struck at noon. Then the master founder of the Blackfriars bell-foundry is found dead *inside* the mouth of his newest bell, though the bell's lip is too narrow to admit a man. The Worshipful Company wants it called an accident before the consecration; the sexton who found him has resigned and taken to sleeping in daylight.
- **Central mystery question:** How did a man get inside his own bell, and why do three parishes keep striking an hour that hasn't come yet?
- **Rational shadow:** Guild sabotage — a rival founder tampering with clock trains and tuning to ruin Blackfriars' reputation before a lucrative cathedral contract; the death an industrial accident rearranged to look uncanny and force a scandal.
- **Occult truth:** The dead founder had rediscovered a suppressed 17th-century tuning — a partial that doesn't fade but *accumulates*. Bells tuned to it resonate with one another across the city and, at sufficient strength, with something under it. The wrong hour is not an error; it is a countdown being rung in from elsewhere. (Faint Mythos echo only: the tuning's interval appears in the estuary soundings of case #1 as a depth ratio — one shared motif, never remarked on in-case.)
- **Primary locations:** The Blackfriars foundry; three parish belfries and their clock rooms; the Worshipful Company's hall; St. Sepulchre's Crypt (the neutral ground where the rival founders will actually talk).
- **Main factions:** Rationalists Circle (an acoustician member is measuring the phenomenon and lying about the results), Court of Smoke (selling belfry access), Lamplighters (their file on the 17th-century suppression is missing pages).
- **Important NPCs:** Widow Constance Aldergate, who now runs the foundry and knew her husband's tuning notes were dangerous; Mr. Pell, the rival founder, guilty of much but not this; Deacon Fry, the sleepless sexton; Professor Ilsa Kraem, Rationalist acoustician with two sets of measurements; "Tallow" Nan, Court steeplekeeper's daughter who hears the bells "answer each other".
- **Clue ideas:** *(physical)* the bell's inner wall — scored from the inside, outward; *(physical)* the founder's tuning forks, one of which will not stop sounding; *(testimony)* Fry's account of what the bell was doing when he found the body; *(testimony)* Nan's map of which steeples "answer"; *(occult)* the 17th-century founding record with the interval struck through in three different centuries' inks; *(deduction-type)* Kraem's honest dataset, if extracted; *(red herring)* Pell's paid tamperer's confession — real sabotage of the *clocks*, unrelated to the death; *(physical)* wax stoppered in the dead man's ears — he knew not to listen to his own work.
- **Key deductions:** (1) `hc-two-crimes` — tamperer's confession + clock trains ⇒ the sabotage and the death are separate crimes (dissolves the red-herring path); (2) `hc-accumulating-partial` — forks + Kraem's data ⇒ each ringing strengthens the next; (3) `hc-the-answered-hour` — Nan's map + founding record ⇒ the 3 a.m. strike is a response, and the consecration peal will complete it (gates the true ending).
- **Major branching choice:** The consecration peal cannot be publicly stopped without ruining the widow. Crack the new bell (Vigor/sabotage, mundane scandal, occult safety), let it ring with the tuning corrected (requires the true deduction), or let it ring true and *listen* (Nerve, Composure catastrophe, unique knowledge).
- **Faculty emphasis:** Perception and Lore lead (this is the sensory case); Nerve high; Reason for the two-crimes untangling; Vigor for belfry work; Influence for the Company and the crypt negotiation.
- **Archetype opportunities:** Occultist — the only archetype who can read the struck-through interval safely; Operator — belfry climbs and the bell-cracking path; Deductionist — separating the two crimes; Mesmerist — extracting Kraem's honest dataset.
- **Flags / reputation / unlocks:** `hc-bell-cracked` / `hc-peal-heard` persist; Rationalists rep hinges on how Kraem is handled; first use of St. Sepulchre's Crypt sets `visited-sepulchre` for reuse elsewhere.
- **Why it adds something new:** First sound-driven mystery (clue patterns are aural, not visual); activates St. Sepulchre's Crypt; the impossible-body-in-the-bell is a fresh impossibility distinct from the existing locked room.

### 4. The Cartographer's Fever
- **Id slug:** `the-cartographers-fever`
- **Type:** case (3 acts)
- **Synopsis:** Two Ordnance Survey draughtsmen have died of "brain fever" within a month, and a third has barricaded himself in the map room of a Holborn survey office, redrawing the same sheet over and over. Each revision includes a street that does not exist — Candlewick Turn — drawn with more confidence each time. The Survey wants discretion. The landlady of the barricaded man wants him fed. And a property syndicate has, very quietly, begun buying the houses that would adjoin Candlewick Turn if it were real.
- **Central mystery question:** Is Candlewick Turn a delusion spreading between draughtsmen — or a place, and if so, what is buying its neighbours?
- **Rational shadow:** Property fraud — the syndicate is seeding a fake street into official sheets to manufacture a right-of-way claim over land it wants cheap; the "fever" is arsenical pigment poisoning from a bad batch of map ink; the third draughtsman is simply the sickest.
- **Occult truth:** The street is real intermittently. The survey's new triangulation crossed a seam where London's geometry doesn't close, and drawing the seam accurately *widens* it. The syndicate's principal has walked Candlewick Turn and wants to own both ends of a door. Impossible-architecture Mythos anchor: what is glimpsed *down* the Turn matches the drowned streets drawn at Ravenscroft (case #2) and the estuary structure (case #1) — three motifs, one pattern, never stated.
- **Primary locations:** The Holborn survey office and its map room; the terrace where Candlewick Turn should be; the syndicate's solicitors; the ink supplier's works; the Whispering Gallery (to buy an older, honest map).
- **Main factions:** Court of Smoke (muscle for the syndicate's purchases), Hermetic Order of the Grey Dawn (they have a 1790s sheet showing the Turn and will trade for confirmation it has reopened), Rationalists Circle (the arsenic explanation is theirs, and half-right).
- **Important NPCs:** Mr. Nathaniel Quill, the barricaded draughtsman, lucid about everything except the street; Mrs. Beade, his landlady and the case's conscience; Sir Edmund Vale, syndicate principal, who carries a pebble he will not explain; Miss Harriet Okafor, Survey computer (mathematician) who found the triangulation error and was reassigned for it; Mr. Dree, the Grey Dawn's map-dealer in the Whispering Gallery.
- **Clue ideas:** *(physical)* the three revisions of the sheet — the Turn gains a door number each time; *(physical)* the ink assay — genuinely arsenical; *(testimony)* Okafor's suppressed triangulation memorandum; *(testimony)* a lamplighter (lowercase — a literal one) who swears he has lit a lamp on a street he can never find again by day; *(occult)* Vale's pebble — basalt, angular-marked, identical to the sounding-weight of case #1; *(occult)* the 1790s Grey Dawn sheet showing the Turn *and its closure annotation*; *(red herring)* the syndicate's forged right-of-way deed — real fraud, prepared for a street they believed fake; *(deduction-type)* Quill's door numbers, which are dates.
- **Key deductions:** (1) `cf-ink-and-error` — assay + memorandum ⇒ both the mundane and the strange explanations are simultaneously true and neither is sufficient; (2) `cf-the-turn-opens` — revisions + lamplighter testimony ⇒ drawing it widens it; (3) `cf-vale-has-walked-it` — pebble + deed ⇒ Vale's fraud became something else after he went inside (gates the true ending).
- **Major branching choice:** Quill's final sheet is nearly finished and Vale wants it. Burn the sheet and Okafor's figures (the seam narrows, Quill is ruined as a madman), complete the sheet under controlled conditions with the Grey Dawn (they close it as it was closed in the 1790s — for a price), or walk the Turn before it closes.
- **Faculty emphasis:** Reason leads strongly (the paper case is dense); Perception for the terrace scenes; Lore for the Grey Dawn material; Influence for Vale and the solicitors; Nerve/Vigor concentrated in the Turn itself.
- **Archetype opportunities:** Deductionist — the date/door-number cipher is theirs to crack; Occultist — Veil Sight on the terrace shows where the Turn will be *tonight*; Mesmerist — Vale's pebble story, extracted; Operator — the syndicate's strongroom for the deed.
- **Flags / reputation / unlocks:** `cf-turn-walked` / `cf-turn-closed` persist; Grey Dawn rep +2 on the controlled-closure path (this is the intended route to unlocking vignette #8 for players who lack it); sets `mythos-angular-mark` on connecting the pebble to anything.
- **Why it adds something new:** First case about *place itself* as the mystery; heaviest Reason/document case since Whitechapel but with a wholly different texture (maps and mathematics, not ciphers); gives the Grey Dawn a transactional, unsettling on-screen role.

### 5. The Comet Club
- **Id slug:** `the-comet-club`
- **Type:** case (3 acts)
- **Synopsis:** The Aldebaran Society — twelve gentleman-astronomers who have dined together since observing the 1882 comet — is dying in seating order. Three members in five weeks, each death natural on its face: heart, fall, fever. The Society's remaining fellows have rewritten their wills, hired food-tasters, and gone on meeting, because the club's founding wager binds the survivor to inherit the private observatory in Hampstead and everything its dome conceals. The eighth chair hires the investigator as his guest — placing the player *inside* the seating order.
- **Central mystery question:** Is someone murdering along the seating chart for the tontine — or did all twelve see something through the great refractor in 1882 that is now collecting them in the order they looked?
- **Rational shadow:** A tontine murder plot — the classic. One member (or an heir) is engineering plausible deaths; the seating-order pattern is a deliberate theatrical flourish to spook the survivors into reckless will changes.
- **Occult truth:** Both. There *is* a murderer working the tontine — responsible for exactly one of the deaths, hiding inside the pattern. The pattern itself is older: the 1882 observation logged an object *between* the comet and the Earth, occulting stars that should not have flickered, and each man who took the eyepiece received, in effect, a numbered ticket. Astronomical-anomaly Mythos anchor: the object's computed period matches the return dates annotated in three different inks in case #3's founding record.
- **Primary locations:** The Society's Pall Mall dining rooms; the Hampstead observatory and its sealed dome annex; members' homes (three death scenes); the Royal Astronomical Society's library.
- **Main factions:** Rationalists Circle (several members overlap — the Circle wants the 1882 log destroyed as an embarrassment), Lamplighters (one of the dead was their consulting astronomer), Court of Smoke (a member's heir has debts, which is where the real murderer was recruited).
- **Important NPCs:** Colonel Aster, the eighth chair, the player's client — brave, dying anyway, and honest about neither; Lady Wren Halloway, widow of chair three, who has read her husband's observation diary; Mr. Pettifer Sloane, chair eleven, the tontine murderer's employer's… or is he; Dr. Ost, Society astronomer who has recomputed the 1882 object's period and stopped sleeping on nights it would be overhead; Briggs, the observatory caretaker, who oils a telescope no one is permitted to use.
- **Clue ideas:** *(physical)* chair five's "fall" — sash weights recently moved, a genuine murder's genuine evidence; *(physical)* the 1882 observation log, with one page razored out; *(testimony)* Lady Halloway on her husband's last weeks — he slept in daylight (echoing case #3's sexton, a deliberate cross-case motif); *(testimony)* Briggs on which nights the dome is "worked" though no one enters; *(occult)* the razored page, recovered — the sketch of the occulting object is an angular mark (the recurring motif); *(occult)* Ost's recomputation: the object's period, and the fact that the next perihelion is not far off; *(red herring)* chair two's poisoned tonic — self-administered quackery, natural death after all; *(deduction-type)* the seating chart against the death dates: the interval is shrinking.
- **Key deductions:** (1) `cc-one-true-murder` — sash weights + Sloane's finances ⇒ exactly one death is human work (unmasks the tontine plot); (2) `cc-numbered-tickets` — seating chart + Halloway diary ⇒ the pattern predates the murderer, who is exploiting it; (3) `cc-the-return` — razored page + Ost's period ⇒ what they saw is periodic, and the club's deaths are its approach (gates the true ending).
- **Major branching choice:** The night of the next "collection", the surviving members gather at the observatory — Aster intends to take the eyepiece deliberately and see it *back*. Expose the human murderer and disperse the club (the pattern continues, unobserved), let Aster look while the player observes him (Nerve; his ticket is punched but the player gains the case's deepest knowledge), or take the eyepiece yourself.
- **Faculty emphasis:** Reason and Influence lead (a drawing-room case); Perception at the death scenes; Lore for the astronomy; Nerve peaks at the dome; Vigor via a rooftop pursuit of the human murderer.
- **Archetype opportunities:** Deductionist — the one-true-murder separation is the series' purest deduction set-piece; Mesmerist — the dining-room interrogations under club courtesy; Occultist — reading the razored sketch without the dome; Operator — the rooftop pursuit and the dome annex's sealed hatch.
- **Flags / reputation / unlocks:** `cc-took-the-eyepiece` / `cc-club-dispersed` persist; sets `mythos-period-computed` if Ost's figure is obtained (a load-bearing Mythos flag — see Part 4); unlocks vignette #10.
- **Why it adds something new:** First closed-circle/And-Then-There-Were-None structure; puts the player *inside* the victim pattern (personal stakes without a kidnapped ally cliché); the double-solution (one human murder inside an inhuman pattern) is a structure no existing case uses.

### 6. A Season of Masks
- **Id slug:** `a-season-of-masks`
- **Type:** case (3 acts)
- **Synopsis:** At the Duchess of Brayle's masked ball — the event of the Season — a debutante walks into the cloakroom and does not walk out; her mask is found on a woman who has worn it all evening in full view of forty witnesses. Within days, three of society's most careful people receive letters quoting words they spoke *only behind their masks*. The Duchess engages the investigator to find the girl and stop the letters before the Season — and several fortunes and engagements balanced upon it — collapses.
- **Central mystery question:** Who is behind the blackmail, where is the missing debutante, and how does the blackmailer hear what masks conceal?
- **Rational shadow:** A blackmail ring with excellent tradecraft — servants as listeners, a duplicate-mask switch in the cloakroom, and the debutante a runaway (or recruit) rather than a victim. Everything the letters quote could have been overheard by a well-placed footman.
- **Occult truth (suspected):** The maskmaker — a Court of Smoke atelier — has begun lacquering masks with a preparation that *retains*: worn long enough, a mask remembers the face and the words behind it, and can be "played back" by one who knows how. The debutante discovered it and is being kept because of what her mask now holds. The case can resolve with the player never certain the retention is real — the strongest deniability in the catalog; no Mythos content at all (this case is the control, proving unrelated occult business continues in London).
- **Primary locations:** Brayle House; the Court atelier off the Whispering Gallery; a Belgravia modiste's; the servants' registry office; a pawnshop where a mask surfaces (light echo of The Unfinished Case's opening, one scene only).
- **Main factions:** Court of Smoke (the atelier, and the ring — but its leadership did not sanction this, which is leverage), Hermetic Order of the Grey Dawn (wants the retention preparation), Lamplighters (a Lamplighter's own indiscretion is in the letters, compromising the player's usual ally).
- **Important NPCs:** The Duchess of Brayle, client, blackmail victim, and plausible suspect all at once; Miss Cordelia Vane-Tempest, the missing debutante (found at the midpoint — the case then becomes about what to do with her); Maestro Lucan Sorrell, the maskmaker, artist first and criminal a distant second; Inspector Cutts, the compromised Lamplighter; "Feathers", the cloakroom attendant who performed the switch and knows she performed only half of it.
- **Clue ideas:** *(testimony)* Feathers' account — she swapped one mask, but two are missing; *(physical)* the duplicate mask, seams machine-perfect except one hand-cut flaw; *(physical)* the letters — each quotes speech with small errors, as if *heard through lacquer*; *(testimony)* the modiste on who commissioned duplicates, and for whom; *(occult)* a "spent" mask from the atelier's rejects bin — warm at the mouth, and the player's Lore may hear a syllable; *(red herring)* Inspector Cutts' payments to the registry office — covering his indiscretion, not running the ring; *(deduction-type)* the seating of the forty witnesses against the letters' quotes: three quotes could not have been overheard by any servant; *(physical)* Cordelia's dance card, filled out in two hands.
- **Key deductions:** (1) `sm-two-masks` — Feathers + duplicate ⇒ the switch concealed a second theft; (2) `sm-no-listener` — seating chart + letters ⇒ at least three quotes had no possible human ear (the case's deniability hinge — this deduction is *optional* and the case resolves mundanely without it); (3) `sm-cordelia-chose` — dance card + modiste ⇒ the debutante commissioned her own duplicate (gates the best ending).
- **Major branching choice:** Cordelia will not come home: her mask holds a confession that would ruin her father and free her. Return her and suppress the mask (the Duchess's outcome), stage her death and let her vanish with the Court (her outcome), or force the confession into the open (justice, and the Season's ruin).
- **Faculty emphasis:** Influence dominant (the most social case in the catalog); Perception for the ballroom reconstructions; Reason for the seating-chart set-piece; Nerve in the atelier; Vigor via one carriage pursuit; Lore optional but rewarded.
- **Archetype opportunities:** Mesmerist — this is *their* case, with an exclusive scene playing a retained mask back; Deductionist — the forty-witness reconstruction; Occultist — sensing which masks in the atelier are "awake"; Operator — the below-stairs investigation as a hired footman.
- **Flags / reputation / unlocks:** `sm-cordelia-freed` / `sm-mask-suppressed` persist; Court rep ±2 (exposing the unsanctioned ring can *raise* Court standing — moral ambiguity by design); Lamplighter rep −1 if Cutts is exposed.
- **Why it adds something new:** First high-society Season setting and first Influence-dominant case (Mayfair Séance is the closest, but its spine is the séance, not society itself); the victim-who-chose structure and the "control case" role in the Mythos staging are both new.

### 7. The Tidewaiter's Log
- **Id slug:** `the-tidewaiters-log`
- **Type:** vignette (2 acts)
- **Synopsis:** A customs tidewaiter at the Wapping legal quays keeps an exemplary log — which is why it matters that it records the brig *Speedwell* arriving on the 4th and again, identically, on the 11th: same master, same manifest, same tide. The Customs House calls it a clerical error and has suspended him. He appeals to the investigator: he remembers boarding her twice, and the second time, the crew remembered him.
- **Central mystery question:** Did the same ship arrive twice — and which arrival was the *Speedwell*?
- **Rational shadow:** Smuggling by doppelgänger — a second brig, repainted and re-papered to run contraband through a quay whose paperwork had already cleared; the tidewaiter suspended precisely because his log was too good.
- **Occult truth / Mythos connection:** The second arrival's cargo included estuary dredge-spoil from a channel-deepening works — and the *Speedwell* that arrived on the 11th had sailed *no intervening voyage her crew can account for*. Strong Mythos signal (the strongest to this point): her master's private log for the missing week describes soundings over a structure, in phrasing matching the suppressed 1850s survey of case #1.
- **Primary locations:** The legal quays and Customs House; the *Speedwell* at her mooring; a Rotherhithe breaker's yard (where the "other" brig should be); the tidewaiter's lodgings.
- **Main factions:** Court of Smoke (the smuggling layer is real and theirs), Lamplighters (quietly interested the moment dredge-spoil is mentioned).
- **Important NPCs:** Mr. Ezekiel Tarrant, the tidewaiter, a man whose whole self is his accuracy; Captain Voss, the *Speedwell*'s master, who will not open his private log while sober; "Ropewalk" Sally, Court fixer for the quays.
- **Clue ideas:** *(physical)* the two log entries, letter-identical down to a blot; *(testimony)* Tarrant on the crew greeting him by name on the "first" meeting; *(physical)* the *Speedwell*'s hull — barnacle growth wrong for one week in port; *(occult)* Voss's private log, the missing week; *(red herring)* the breaker's-yard twin brig — real, and repainted for an unrelated fraud.

## Part 3 — Detailed Pitch: The Comet Club

### Act I — The Eighth Chair (THE SCENE)
- Colonel Aster engages the investigator over dinner *at the club* — the player is introduced to all nine survivors in one bravura scene (automatic clues: the seating chart; the club's founding wager; the fact that the deaths run in seating order). Aster is chair eight; he seats the player as his guest at the empty chair three.
- First major choice — where to begin: the most recent death scene (chair five's "fall" — Perception/Vigor path, physical evidence), the widow of chair three (Lady Halloway — Influence/Reason path, testimony and the diary), or the RAS library's copy of the Society's published 1882 observations (Lore/Reason path, notices the published record is *thinner* than the club's private log should be).
- Act I closes with the shrinking-interval realization (a light, un-gated deduction from death dates + seating chart): whatever the mechanism, chair six has less time than chair five had.

### Act II — The Seating Order (THE WEB)
- Full loop, run twice. Loop one, the tontine layer: Sloane's debts, the Court of Smoke moneylender, the sash weights at chair five's house — culminating in key deduction `cc-one-true-murder` (sash weights + Sloane's finances). Midpoint revelation: exactly one death is human work, which means the *other* deaths still need explaining and the murderer knows the pattern well enough to hide inside it.
- Loop two, the pattern layer: Lady Halloway's diary (her husband slept in daylight); Briggs and the dome "worked" at night; the razored page recovered from the founder's effects (occult clue, Composure cost to study); Ost's recomputation extracted (his exclusive scene has him half-hoping to be debunked — a Rationalist mirror of the player's own tension). Key deduction `cc-numbered-tickets` forms here.
- Encounters: a mundane rooftop pursuit when the human murderer's agent is startled at chair six's house (Vigor/Perception); a supernatural manifestation in the dome annex if the player investigates on a night Ost has marked (Nerve/Lore reaction, dual-axis — the telescope is *warm*).
- Variant hooks: if `wc-court-deal-made` persists from Whitechapel, the Court moneylender treats the player as a known quantity and sells Sloane out cheap; if the player is an Occultist, Veil Sight in the dining room shows which chairs are "punched".

### Act III — Perihelion Night (THE RECKONING)
- The human murderer is confronted first (informed by `cc-one-true-murder`; without it, the confrontation happens on the pattern's timetable and at disadvantage). Their defense is the case's moral hinge: they killed one dying man for money, inside a pattern that was killing them all anyway — "I stole from a fire."
- Then the club gathers at Hampstead for the next collection night. Final choice (see branching above): disperse the club / observe Aster at the eyepiece / take the eyepiece. `cc-the-return` (razored page + Ost's period) gates the full version of the third option.

### Evidence progression
Seating chart & death dates (Act I, automatic) → sash weights + Sloane's ledger (Act II loop 1) → **`cc-one-true-murder`** → Halloway diary + Briggs testimony (Act II loop 2) → **`cc-numbered-tickets`** → razored page + Ost's period (late Act II, both cost something) → **`cc-the-return`** → Act III options fully open. The red herring (chair two's quack tonic) is seeded in Act I and dissolves under `cc-one-true-murder` — connecting it produces a contested thread, teaching the pattern-vs-murder distinction mechanically.

### Suspect web
Sloane (means via the moneylender, motive via debt — guilty of chair five only) · the moneylender's agent (hands, not brain) · Lady Halloway (inherits chair three's stake; innocent, but her diary makes her look prescient) · Briggs (access to everything; loyal to the dome, not the men) · Aster himself (the client engaged the investigator the day *after* quietly buying chair eleven's debts — an Act III sting if the player checked).

### Red herring path
Chair two's tonic reads as poisoning (physical clue, plausible connections to Sloane). Players who build a deduction on it get a coherent-but-wrong all-human theory; the Act III confrontation staged on that theory lets the real murderer walk and forfeits the pattern layer — the "compromised by overconfidence" lane the catalog's red-herring rule calls for.

### Endings
- **Best** (requires `cc-the-return` + the murderer unmasked): the club dispersed, the log sealed with the Lamplighters, Aster dissuaded — and the player, alone, keeps Ost's date. Quiet, chilling, complete. Sets `cc-club-dispersed` + `mythos-period-computed`.
- **Compromised**: the murderer unmasked but the pattern unexplained (missing deduction 3) — the survivors scatter, the deaths continue at their shrinking interval offstage; or Aster looks, and the player watches a man's ticket punched. Sets partial flags.
- **Failure** (halt or wrong-theory confrontation): the tontine theory collapses at trial, the club closes ranks, and the player is removed from a pattern that now, per one final letter from Briggs, has *ten* chairs. `cc-took-the-eyepiece` reserved for the deliberate player choice, not failure.

## Part 4 — Staging the Mythos Thread

**The pattern (writer-facing only, never stated in content):** something old and periodic lies under the Thames estuary; its approach is legible three ways — in depth (soundings, drowned architecture), in time (the computed period, the shrinking intervals), and in mind (dreams, excised memories, downstream sight). London's institutions have been erasing the evidence for decades without coordination or comprehension.

**Recurring motifs (the connective tissue):**
- *The angular mark* — on the sounding-weight (#1), Vale's pebble (#4), the razored 1882 sketch (#5). Never named, never translated.
- *Drowned streets* — Ravenscroft registry drawings (#2), what is glimpsed down Candlewick Turn (#4), Mr. Nine's notebooks (#9).
- *The period / shrinking interval* — the bell-founding record's three inks (#3), Ost's computation (#5), the orrery gearing (#8), Nine's final dated page (#9).
- *Sleeping in daylight* — the sexton (#3), chair three (#5), Mr. Nine's decade. A pure texture motif: cheap, eerie, deniable.

**Signal strength by content:**
- *Main cases — faint echoes only.* #1 (The Drowned Archive) is the anchor but resolves as fraud; #3, #4, #5 each carry one motif plus a case-local occult truth; #2's echo is a single registry clue; #6 (A Season of Masks) carries **nothing** — the deliberate control proving not everything connects.
- *Vignettes — the meta-plot's spine.* #7 (strong: the survey phrasing recurs), #9 (medium: the dream limb + the future date), #10 (medium: the suppression map + thesis), #8 (**keystone**: the period match that lets the player *name* the pattern).

**Tracking flags & deductions:**
- Breadcrumb flags, set by optional discoveries: `mythos-estuary-survey` (#1), `mythos-shared-drawing` (#2), `mythos-angular-mark` (#4), `mythos-period-computed` (#5), `mythos-dreamer-read` (#9), `mythos-suppression-mapped` (#10).
- Keystone key deduction `mythos-pattern-named`, mintable only in The Orrery Room (#8) with `mythos-period-computed` held — the engine's existing `hasFlag`-gated choice + `KeyDeduction` recipe machinery covers this with no new mechanics.
- Downstream, any content can gate a variant scene or choice on `hasDeduction: mythos-pattern-named` — e.g. Mr. Nine greets such a player differently; the Grey Dawn's price changes; a future case's ending shelf extends.
- NPC memory flags carry the human side: Persk, Ost (pre-#10), Voss, and Dree can each `setMemoryFlag` on Mythos-adjacent conversations, so the *people* remember what the player asked even when the player lacks the flags.

**Keeping it optional:** every Mythos clue is (a) never on a critical path, (b) always shadowed by a sufficient mundane explanation inside its own case, and (c) discovered via optional exploration/check discoveries or endings the player must choose. A player who ignores the thread completely gets seven-to-ten full mysteries and never notices an absence; a player who chases it assembles, across four vignettes and five faint echoes, the shape of something that is never named, never seen, and — by the final dated page in Room Nine — not finished arriving.

**Suggested build order for the thread:** #5 The Comet Club (mints the period) → #8 The Orrery Room (keystone, unlockable immediately after) → #1 The Drowned Archive → #7 The Tidewaiter's Log → remainder in any order. This front-loads the two highest-ranked builds and makes the thread playable end-to-end after only four pieces of content.
